# Sustainability and Community Impact Explanations

## Overview

Simalem wants guests to understand not only what they will do, but also what their choices may support or affect. This means translating sustainability and community impact into practical, guest-friendly language.

## Community Impact Explained

Community impact refers to whether an activity creates visible benefits for local people, local partners, or local livelihoods.

### High-value community impact patterns

These are activities more likely to create visible local benefit:

- guided cultural experiences with local guides
- artisan classes taught by local craftspeople
- food or agricultural experiences involving local families or farmers
- experiences where guest spending directly reaches community partners

### Lower-value community impact patterns

These are activities where the guest experience is the main value, but there is limited clear local livelihood benefit:

- self-contained resort activities
- generic thrill activities with limited local participation
- experiences where the money flow to community members is weak or unclear

## Environmental Impact Explained

Environmental impact refers to the likely relative footprint of the activity.

Factors that may increase impact:

- motorized transport or equipment
- ecological disturbance
- high resource consumption
- activity intensity in sensitive natural areas

Factors that may reduce impact:

- low-energy formats
- slower paced activities
- educational and stewardship-oriented experiences
- small-group guided experiences with lower disturbance

## Important Framing Rule

Environmental impact in this project is relative, not absolute.
The label should be interpreted as:

- lower compared with other available activities
- higher compared with other available activities

It should not be framed as a scientific carbon accounting result unless evidence exists.

## Examples of Clear Explanations

### Direct Local Partner

“This experience directly supports local partners such as artisans, farmers, guides, or families.”

### Internal Community Support

“This experience contributes to broader stewardship or community-supporting efforts, even if the benefit is less direct.”

### No Direct Community Link

“This experience currently has limited direct, traceable benefit to local community livelihoods.”

### Low Environmental Impact

“This activity has a relatively lighter environmental footprint compared with more resource-intensive options.”

### High Environmental Impact

“This activity may create a greater environmental footprint, and we surface that so guests can make an informed choice.”

## Trust Rule

If an activity is exciting but weaker on transparency dimensions, the system should still recommend it when appropriate, but with honest wording.

Example:
“This activity strongly matches your interest in adventure. It currently has limited direct community benefit and a higher environmental footprint than some other options.”

## Education Rule

Transparency should educate without overwhelming the user.
The system should not produce long lectures unless asked.
It should explain enough for informed choice.

## What Not to Do

Do not:

- exaggerate impact
- imply certainty without evidence
- use guilt-heavy language
- pretend all recommended activities are equally sustainable
- hide weaker-performing activities from the user
