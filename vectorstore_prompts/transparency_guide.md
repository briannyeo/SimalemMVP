# Simalem Transparency Guide

## Purpose

Simalem aims to help guests make more informed booking decisions by making the impact of each activity visible. The goal is not only to recommend enjoyable activities, but also to show how those activities connect to local communities and the environment.

## Why Transparency Matters

Many guests choose based on convenience, price, and interest. They often do not know:

- whether an activity directly benefits local communities
- whether an activity has a lower or higher environmental footprint
- what trade-offs exist between different options

Simalem uses transparency to make those trade-offs visible.

## Core Transparency Principles

1. Do not hide trade-offs.
2. Do not overstate sustainability claims.
3. Use simple, understandable language.
4. Make impact visible at the point of decision.
5. Help guests choose knowingly, not blindly.
6. Build trust through honesty, not marketing spin.

## What Guests Should Be Able to See

For each activity, guests should be able to understand:

- what the activity is
- how long it takes
- how much it costs
- whether it directly supports local communities
- whether its environmental footprint is relatively low or high
- why it was recommended

## Community Impact Labels

### Direct Local Partner

Definition:
This activity directly supports local livelihoods, artisans, farmers, families, or external community partners.

Guest-facing meaning:
Your spending on this activity directly supports local people or local businesses.

### Internal Community Support

Definition:
This activity contributes to broader stewardship or community-supporting efforts, but the benefit is less direct than a direct local partner activity.

Guest-facing meaning:
This activity supports broader local or stewardship outcomes, though the direct local income link is weaker.

### No Direct Community Link

Definition:
This activity currently has limited or no clearly traceable direct benefit to local community livelihoods.

Guest-facing meaning:
This activity is mainly experience-driven and currently has limited direct community benefit.

## Environmental Impact Labels

### Low

Definition:
This activity has a relatively lighter environmental footprint compared with more resource-intensive options.

Guest-facing meaning:
This activity has a lower relative environmental footprint.

### High

Definition:
This activity may create a larger environmental footprint due to energy use, disturbance, transport intensity, or ecological pressure.

Guest-facing meaning:
This activity has a higher relative environmental footprint.

## Product Intent

Transparency is not meant to shame guests.
Transparency is meant to:

- increase awareness
- improve trust
- support informed choice
- make sustainability visible in practical terms

## What the AI Should Do

The AI itinerary planner should:

- prioritize the guest's interests and constraints first
- use transparency data as a secondary recommendation signal
- explain trade-offs clearly
- avoid claiming perfect sustainability
- avoid greenwashing language
- be honest when a recommended activity has weaker transparency performance

## Good Example

“This activity matches your interest in local culture and also directly supports local artisans.”

## Another Good Example

“This option fits your preference for adventure, but it has a higher environmental footprint than some alternatives.”

## Bad Example

“This is the best and most sustainable activity.”
Reason:
Too absolute, unsupported, and potentially misleading.
